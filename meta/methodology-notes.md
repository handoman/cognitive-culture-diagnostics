# Methodology Notes

This document provides meta-level clarification of the methodological choices underlying the Cognitive Culture Diagnostics project.

It does not present empirical results or procedural instructions.  
Its purpose is to explain **why certain methodological approaches were chosen**, and how they should be interpreted.

---

## Methodological Orientation

The project is grounded in a **process-oriented, single-subject research logic**.

The primary research interest is not behavioral frequency or symptom prevalence, but:
- internal cognitive architecture,
- representational modes,
- process dynamics,
- and compensatory mechanisms.

This orientation necessitates methodological choices that differ from standard population-based designs.

---

## Self-as-Instrument Design (SAID)

The project uses a **Self-as-Instrument Design (SAID)**.

In this design:
- the subject and the primary analytic instrument are the same individual,
- introspective access is treated as a data source,
- transparency replaces anonymization.

This choice is methodological, not autobiographical.

The subject is not presented as representative, but as **maximally accessible**.

---

## Rationale for Single-Subject Focus

A single-subject design is used because:

- cognitive architecture is difficult to infer from surface behavior alone,
- many compensatory processes are invisible in group-level data,
- misalignment effects emerge most clearly under close inspection.

The case functions as a **diagnostic stress test**, not as a statistical sample.

---

## Introspective Data and Validity

Introspective data is treated as:

- structured,
- bounded,
- and context-dependent.

It is not assumed to be:
- exhaustive,
- infallible,
- or free from bias.

Validity is supported through:
- consistency across contexts,
- convergence with external observations,
- explicit documentation of uncertainty.

Introspection is used where it provides access to otherwise inaccessible processes.

---

## Compensation as a Research Variable

Compensation is not treated as noise or bias.

Instead, it is considered:
- a core feature of certain cognitive architectures,
- a dynamic process that interacts with assessment tools,
- a variable to be documented rather than controlled away.

This reframes compensation from a confound to a **phenomenon of interest**.

---

## Bias and Reflexivity

The project acknowledges inherent bias due to:
- non-anonymity,
- introspective access,
- and prior knowledge of diagnostic frameworks.

Rather than attempting to eliminate bias, the methodology emphasizes:
- explicit reflexivity,
- documentation of assumptions,
- and traceability of interpretive steps.

Bias is managed through transparency, not denial.

---

## Falsifiability and Limits

Claims in the project are falsifiable at the level of:

- internal consistency,
- cross-contextual coherence,
- and explanatory power.

The framework does not claim universal applicability.

Failure cases, mismatches, or alternative explanations are considered:
- informative,
- expected,
- and necessary for refinement.

---

## Ethical Considerations

Ethical implications of SAID include:

- informed consent by the subject (self),
- acceptance of non-anonymity,
- responsibility for accurate self-representation.

These considerations are addressed through:
- explicit scope limitation,
- avoidance of normative claims,
- and separation of description from prescription.

---

## Stability Statement

The methodological orientation described here is considered **stable**.

Future empirical work may:
- extend,
- test,
- or challenge these choices,

but the rationale for their use in this project should remain explicit and unchanged.
