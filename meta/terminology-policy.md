# Terminology Policy

This document defines how key terms are used within the Cognitive Culture Diagnostics project.

Its purpose is to ensure **semantic consistency**, prevent conceptual drift, and clarify how certain terms are reinterpreted or deliberately constrained.

This is not a glossary.  
It is a **policy for language use**.

---

## General Principles

- Terms are used **descriptively**, not normatively.
- Language is chosen to describe **process and architecture**, not value or deficit.
- When common clinical terms are used in non-standard ways, this is intentional and documented.
- No term should be interpreted outside the context of the framework in which it is defined.

---

## Core Terminological Distinctions

### Cognitive Culture vs. Diagnosis

- **Cognitive Culture** refers to how cognition is internally structured.
- It is not a diagnosis, trait, or personality type.
- It does not imply impairment or superiority.

Cognitive culture describes **architecture**, not outcome.

---

### Culture vs. Style

The term *culture* is used deliberately rather than *style*.

- *Style* implies preference or surface-level variation.
- *Culture* implies a coherent, internally consistent system of representation, processing, and governance.

Cognitive culture refers to **deep structural regularities**, not behavioral habits.

---

### Architecture vs. Behavior

- **Architecture** refers to internal organization of cognition.
- **Behavior** refers to observable output.

The project prioritizes architecture as the explanatory level.  
Behavior is treated as a downstream expression.

---

## Reinterpreted Clinical Terms

The following terms are used with **explicitly constrained meanings**:

### Rigiditet (Rigidity)

- In standard diagnostic contexts, rigidity often implies inflexibility.
- In this project, apparent rigidity may reflect:
  - operational consistency,
  - stable internal logic,
  - efficient reuse of cognitive algorithms.

Rigidity is not assumed without testing **speed of recalibration**.

---

### Maskering (Masking)

- Masking typically implies conscious or unconscious suppression of traits.
- In this project, many phenomena described as masking are instead understood as:
  - architectural differences,
  - alternative representational modes,
  - compensatory process structures.

Masking is not assumed unless there is evidence of **suppression**, not merely difference.

---

### Affekt (Affect)

- Affect is not equated with emotional expressiveness.
- Emotional encoding may occur through:
  - narrative,
  - somatic sensation,
  - analytical consequence mapping,
  - intensity signals.

Low affective expression does not imply low affective experience.

---

### Intellektualisering (Intellectualization)

- Intellectualization is not used as a default explanation for analytical language.
- Analytical expression may be the **primary affective language** in certain cognitive cultures.

The term is used only when there is evidence of **avoidance**, not structure.

---

## Use of Diagnostic Labels

- ADHD and AST are used as **clinical categories**, not explanatory models.
- The framework does not redefine diagnostic criteria.
- Diagnostic labels are treated as **classification tools**, not ontological truths.

Interpretation occurs at the level of process, not label.

---

## Stability Statement

The terminology policy defined here is considered **stable**.

New terms may be introduced if:
- they describe previously unarticulated processes,
- they do not conflict with existing definitions,
- their scope is explicitly bounded.

Existing terms should not be redefined without documentation.
