# Structural Architecture of the Monograph

This document describes the **structural design** of the Cognitive Culture Diagnostics monograph and the rationale behind it.

It does not summarize content.  
It explains **why the content is organized the way it is**.

---

## High-Level Structure

The monograph is organized into five main parts, followed by appendices:

1. **Foundational framing**
2. **Theoretical framework**
3. **Applied case analysis**
4. **Clinical translation**
5. **Synthesis, epistemology, and future directions**
6. **Appendices (reference material)**

Each part serves a distinct function and is not interchangeable with the others.

---

## Part I – Framing the Diagnostic Problem

**Purpose:**  
To establish the diagnostic paradox that motivates the project.

This section:
- introduces the mismatch between existing diagnostic tools and certain cognitive profiles,
- frames the problem without proposing solutions,
- prepares the reader for a process-oriented rather than symptom-oriented analysis.

This part is intentionally short and conceptual.

---

## Part II – Theoretical Framework

**Purpose:**  
To define the conceptual tools used throughout the rest of the monograph.

This section introduces:
- Cognitive Culture Framework (CCF),
- Systemic Cognitive Culture (SCC),
- Meta-Logical Cognitive Culture (MLCC),
- a formal differentiation matrix between ADHD + SCC/MLCC and AST.

All later chapters assume familiarity with this framework.  
No new core concepts are introduced after this part.

---

## Part III – Applied Case Analysis

**Purpose:**  
To demonstrate how cognitive culture manifests in practice.

This section:
- applies the framework to a single, transparent subject,
- uses Self-as-Instrument Design (SAID),
- focuses on process, not outcome,
- shows how diagnostic tools interact with cognitive architecture.

The case is not presented as representative or generalizable.  
It is used as a **diagnostic stress test** for existing models.

---

## Part IV – Clinical Translation

**Purpose:**  
To translate theory and case analysis into clinical practice.

This section includes:
- interviewing methodology,
- assessment design principles,
- clinical recommendations,
- revised interpretive heuristics.

The focus is on **how clinicians should adapt tools**, not on replacing them.

---

## Part V – Synthesis and Future Directions

**Purpose:**  
To integrate findings and situate them within a broader scientific context.

This section:
- discusses epistemological implications,
- outlines future diagnostic systems,
- proposes research directions,
- summarizes theoretical, clinical, and societal consequences.

No new empirical claims are introduced here.

---

## Appendices

**Purpose:**  
To provide reference material and practical tools without interrupting the main argument.

Appendices include:
- terminology and glossary,
- clinical checklists,
- mapping sheets,
- visual model descriptions,
- common misalignment patterns,
- pedagogical models.

Appendices are designed to be usable independently of the main text.

---

## Structural Design Principles

The following principles guided all structural decisions:

- **Separation of argument and reference**  
  Core reasoning remains in chapters; tools and definitions are placed in appendices.

- **One function per chapter**  
  Chapters are internally coherent and not subdivided into modular fragments.

- **Progressive abstraction**  
  The text moves from concrete diagnostic problems to abstract synthesis.

- **No recursive restructuring**  
  Once a concept is introduced and defined, it is not redefined later.

---

## Stability Statement

The structural architecture described here is considered **fixed**.

Future revisions may:
- clarify content,
- expand examples,
- refine language,

but should not:
- split chapters,
- merge parts,
- relocate appendices into the main text,
- or reorder the core progression.

Any structural change should be explicitly documented in `versioning-and-status.md`.
